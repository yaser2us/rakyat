The Transcript — Chapter One: Page One

Selin came on a Thursday evening.
She had sixty-seven messages.
She wanted to be told she was imagining it.

経緯 — context

She spread the pages flat.

She had a stack of paper under her arm and that specific look — the look women get in their mid-forties when they suspect they are making something from nothing. Not alarmed. Not quite. Somewhere between confused and embarrassed.

"I printed it out," she said. "Sixty-seven messages. I don't know. Something feels wrong but I can't — I just can't name it."

The gesture was the first thing I noticed. Not the pages themselves — the way someone lays out evidence they are not yet sure is evidence.

I am a dating columnist. I have read a great many messages. I picked up page one and told myself I would notice anything unusual within thirty seconds.

"He seems fun," I said. Because that was what I saw.

Twenty minutes later I had turned back to page one and started again. I was looking for a single fact about Wong that I could write on a piece of paper and verify. There were none. Not evasive none. Not deflected none. Warm, comfortable none. Sixty-seven messages and nothing.

証拠物 — the artifact

The transcript, examined.

I contacted Farah. She had worked as a forensic linguist for eleven years, consulted on fraud cases. I asked if she would read it with me. She arrived at nine. She read the transcript standing at my kitchen counter — one finger moving slowly down each page, stopping for nothing. Eight minutes.

When she finished she set it down. Then, without explanation, she picked up page one.

異常 — the anomaly

He never answered a single question.

The anomaly can be stated in two numbers.

Forty-one. Zero.

Forty-one questions from Selin. Not one biographical fact returned from Wong. Not evasive — warm. Sixty-seven messages across twenty-one pages, and nothing I could write on a piece of paper and hand to someone.

A man who deflects questions. Guarded, perhaps. A little mysterious. There are ordinary explanations — he values his privacy, he moves slowly with people, he's been hurt before. None of this is alarming. Some of it is even charming. "He seems fun," I said.
Re-read from the beginning knowing what the questions were for. Notice that every question Selin asked about him was answered with a question back to her. Notice that he never once corrected her, interrupted her, or gave her information she hadn't asked for. Notice that the asymmetry is not accidental — it is structural. Someone designed this.
"A tic is one phrase. Maybe two. This is six phrases. In the same order. Every time. He varies the punctuation. The timing. The emoji. The sequence does not vary." Farah drew a thin line connecting the circles. "I'm saying this formed around a purpose, not a feeling."
FarahIn interrogation training, a rotation like this has a name. A redirect protocol. Six deflections cover every conversational direction a target might take. You practise until the cycling feels natural. Until it sounds like personality.

Farah read the bracketed questions aloud, in order. Her pen moved. A column. Arrows connecting each entry to the next. When I finished reading them to her, she set down her pen.

啓示 — revelation

He was after her mother.

We called Selin. Farah asked whether Wong had ever asked anything that felt, in retrospect, slightly specific. Slightly out of place.

Selin remembered two things without hesitating. He had asked about her father — after she mentioned he had passed. His full name. His birthday. She had thought it was sweet. And the bank question. In passing, just like that.

FarahDid he ever ask about your mother's maiden name?

Silence on the line.

SelinYes. I told him my grandfather's family name — from the story about my grandmother, the one I was named after. He said he wanted to picture her. I didn't think of that as — I didn't realise that was the name.

Farah looked at the extracted questions. At the six circled phrases. At Selin's warm, detailed answers filling every page of the right-hand margin.

Outside, the radio downstairs was playing something with strings. I had never noticed it before.

Farah opened her notepad. She wrote one word. She turned it to face me.

The clinical term for a practiced extraction technique.

Elicitation.
